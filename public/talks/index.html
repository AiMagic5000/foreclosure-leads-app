Part 1
Foundations

1. Purpose of This Guide

More of the claims coming through the pipeline now involve properties tied to deceased persons or owned by business entities (LLCs, corporations, trusts). These situations are different from a standard owner-of-record claim in two important ways:

  1. The person you are speaking with is not the original owner. They are a spouse, child, sibling, executor, corporate officer, or authorized agent. The conversation requires a different tone and a different set of questions.
  2. The paperwork is heavier. States require additional documentation to prove the claimant's legal authority to collect. One missing document can delay or kill a claim.

This guide gives you the exact words to say, the exact documents to collect, and the exact steps to follow so that every sensitive claim is handled professionally, respectfully, and successfully.

Mandatory Reading Every agent -- junior, partner, and owner-operator -- must read this guide completely before working any claim involving a deceased property owner or a company-owned property. No exceptions.

3. Core Communication Principles

Every call, email, and letter you send reflects on Foreclosure Recovery Inc. and on this entire profession. These five principles are non-negotiable:

Principle 1: Lead With Empathy, Follow With Facts

When you reach out to a family that has lost someone, the surplus funds are the last thing on their mind. Acknowledge the human reality before you talk about money. A 10-second pause to express genuine condolence costs you nothing and earns you everything.

Principle 2: You Are Not a Salesperson

You are a recovery specialist. You are calling because unclaimed money exists and the state is not going to hand it over voluntarily. Your job is to inform, not to pitch. If someone does not want help, thank them and move on. Pressure tactics destroy credibility and invite complaints.

Principle 3: Clarity Over Cleverness

Use plain English. Avoid legal jargon unless you are defining a term for the person you are speaking with. If someone does not understand what "letters testamentary" means, explain it simply: "That is the document the court gives you that says you have the legal authority to handle the estate." Never make someone feel dumb for asking a question.

Principle 4: Document Everything

Every call gets a note in the CRM. Every promise you make gets written down. Every document you request gets tracked. If you told someone you would call back Thursday at 2 PM, you call back Thursday at 2 PM. Reliability is your competitive advantage.

Principle 5: When In Doubt, Escalate

If a situation feels off -- the heir is hostile, the corporate structure is tangled, there is a potential fraud indicator, or you simply are not sure what to do -- stop and escalate to your team lead or to Foreclosure Recovery Inc. directly at (888) 545-8007. There is no penalty for asking. There are consequences for guessing wrong.

4. The Golden Rules

Rule 1: Never Say "County" Always say "state" instead. We say "the state holds the funds" -- never "the county holds the funds." We say "file with the state" -- never "file with the county." This is company policy across all communications. No exceptions.
Rule 2: Always Say "We" -- Never "I" You speak for the company, not for yourself. "We can help you recover these funds" -- never "I can help you." "We handle all the paperwork" -- never "I handle it." This positions us as a professional organization, not a solo operator.
Rule 3: Never Provide Legal Advice We are asset recovery specialists, not attorneys. If a claimant asks a question that requires legal interpretation -- inheritance disputes, will contests, corporate dissolution law -- direct them to consult their own attorney. You can say: "That is a legal question that we are not qualified to answer. We would recommend speaking with an attorney who handles estate or business law in your state."
Rule 4: Verify Before You Speak Before making any outreach call, confirm the surplus amount, property address, and sale date from your records. Getting basic facts wrong on a call kills your credibility instantly, especially when the person on the other end is grieving.
Part 2
Deceased Owner Claims

5. Understanding Deceased Owner Claims

A deceased owner claim happens when the person who owned the property at the time of the foreclosure sale has passed away. The surplus funds still exist and are still recoverable -- but the original owner is no longer alive to claim them.

This creates a chain-of-authority question: Who has the legal right to claim these funds?

The answer depends on whether the estate went through probate, whether a will exists, and the laws of the state where the property was located.

Why These Claims Require Extra Care

  • You are contacting people during or after a period of grief
  • The person may not know surplus funds exist
  • Family dynamics can be complicated -- siblings may disagree, estranged relatives may surface
  • The documentation burden is higher and mistakes cause delays measured in months
  • Some heirs may be suspicious of unsolicited calls about money connected to a deceased relative
The Upside Deceased owner claims often involve larger surplus amounts because the property may have had significant equity. Heirs who successfully recover these funds are often deeply grateful because they had no idea the money existed. These are some of the most rewarding claims you will work.

6. Identifying the Right Contact

Before you pick up the phone, you need to determine who has the legal standing to authorize a claim. This is the single most important step. Filing a claim with the wrong person's signature wastes everyone's time and can get the claim denied.

Decision Tree: Who Do We Contact?
Was there a will?
YES -- Was probate opened?
YES: Contact the Executor (named in the will and appointed by the court). They have full authority to act for the estate. This is your ideal contact.
NO (probate was never opened): Contact the person named as executor in the will. They will likely need to open probate or obtain a small estate affidavit before we can file. Guide them on what the state requires.
NO -- No will exists (intestate):
Was an administrator appointed by the court?
YES: Contact the Administrator. They serve the same role as an executor but were appointed by the court because there was no will.
NO: Contact the next of kin (spouse first, then children, then siblings -- in that priority order). They will need to either open probate or file a small estate affidavit.
Contact Type Legal Authority Key Document Needed
Executor Named in will, confirmed by court Letters Testamentary
Administrator Court-appointed (no will) Letters of Administration
Surviving Spouse Statutory heir (priority #1 in most states) Marriage certificate + Death certificate
Adult Child Heir (priority #2) Birth certificate + Death certificate
Sibling Heir (if no spouse/children) Birth certificates showing same parent + Death certificate

7. First Contact Scripts -- Deceased Owner Claims

Before You Dial Confirm the surplus amount, property address, and sale date. Research whether the decedent's name matches the property records. Have the lead record open in front of you. Take a breath. This is a sensitive call.

Script A: Cold Call to Potential Heir (You Believe the Owner is Deceased)

Opening -- Warm & Professional

Agent: "Hi, may I speak with [Heir's Name]?"

[Wait for confirmation]

Agent: "[Heir's Name], my name is [Your Name] and we are calling from Foreclosure Recovery Inc. We are an asset recovery firm and we have located what appear to be unclaimed surplus funds tied to a property at [Property Address]. The records show [Deceased's Name] as the former owner. Are you familiar with this property?"

[Pause -- let them respond. Do NOT rush past this.]

If They Confirm the Owner Has Passed Away

Agent: "We are sorry to hear about the passing of [Deceased's Name]. We certainly do not want to intrude during a difficult time. The reason for the call is that after the property sold, there were surplus funds generated from the sale -- money above and beyond what was owed. Those funds are currently being held by the state, and in our experience they will sit there indefinitely unless someone files a formal claim to recover them."

[Pause]

Agent: "What we do is handle the entire recovery process for families in this situation. We prepare all the paperwork, file the claim, and follow up until the funds are released. There is no upfront cost -- we only get paid if the recovery is successful. Would you be open to hearing a bit more about how the process works?"

If They React Emotionally or Seem Upset

Agent: "We completely understand, and please know that we are not trying to add stress to an already difficult situation. These funds belong to [Deceased's Name]'s estate, and by extension to the heirs. We just want to make sure the family is aware that this money exists. If now is not a good time, we are happy to follow up at a time that works better for you. There is no rush and no pressure."

[If they want you to call back, get a specific day and time. Write it down. Honor it.]

If They Are Interested -- Transition to Qualification

Agent: "That is great. Let me ask a few quick questions so we can understand the situation and make sure we can move forward."

Agent: "What was your relationship to [Deceased's Name]?"

Agent: "Do you know if [Deceased's Name]'s estate went through probate?"

Agent: "Was there a will, and if so, was an executor named?"

Agent: "Are there other heirs or family members who might be involved in the estate?"

[Record all answers carefully. These determine your document requirements.]

Script B: Follow-Up Call (They Asked You to Call Back)

Re-Engagement Script

Agent: "Hi [Heir's Name], this is [Your Name] from Foreclosure Recovery Inc. We spoke on [date] about the surplus funds tied to the property at [Address]. You asked us to follow up today. Is this still a good time to talk?"

[If yes, pick up where you left off. If no, reschedule again -- calmly, patiently.]

8. Handling Emotional Conversations

You will encounter grief, anger, confusion, and suspicion. Sometimes all in the same call. Here is how to handle each.

Grief

Do This

  • "We are sorry for your loss."
  • "Take all the time you need."
  • "We can absolutely call back another day."
  • Lower your voice slightly. Slow your pace.
  • Allow silence. Do not try to fill every pause.

Never Do This

  • "I know how you feel." (You do not.)
  • Immediately pivot to the money.
  • Sound rushed or transactional.
  • Say "at least" anything ("at least there is money...")
  • Minimize the loss in any way.

Suspicion / "Is This a Scam?"

Handling Scam Concerns Directly

Agent: "That is a completely fair question, and honestly, we are glad you asked. There are scammers out there and you should be cautious. Here is how you can verify everything we are telling you:"

"First, the surplus funds are a matter of public record. You can look up [Property Address] through the state's records to confirm that excess funds exist from the sale."

"Second, Foreclosure Recovery Inc. is a registered company. You can look us up at usforeclosurerecovery.com or call our main office at (888) 545-8007."

"Third, we never ask for upfront money. We work on contingency -- if we do not recover the funds, you owe us nothing. There is zero financial risk to you."

"Would you like a moment to verify any of that before we continue?"

Anger / Hostility

De-Escalation Response

Agent: "We understand this is a difficult situation, and the last thing we want to do is add to that. We are simply reaching out because these funds belong to the estate, and we believe the family should know about them. If you would prefer not to pursue this, that is completely your right. We respect that decision."

[If they continue to be hostile, end the call politely: "We appreciate your time. If anything changes, you can reach us at (888) 545-8007. Have a good day." -- Then mark the lead accordingly in the CRM.]

Confusion / Overwhelmed

Simplification Response

Agent: "Let us simplify this. When the property at [Address] was sold, it sold for more than what was owed. That extra money -- the surplus -- is sitting with the state right now. As an heir, you have a legal right to claim it. What we do is handle all the paperwork and filing so you do not have to figure it out on your own. We only charge a fee if the money is successfully recovered. Does that make sense so far?"

[Break the process into small, digestible pieces. Never dump the entire process on someone who is already overwhelmed.]

9. Documents Required -- Deceased Owner Claims

Missing Documents = Denied Claims Do not submit a claim until every required document is in hand. A partially assembled claim package signals to the state that you are unprepared, and some states will reject the claim outright rather than ask for supplemental documents.

9.1 Standard Documents (Required for ALL Deceased Owner Claims)

  • Signed Contingency Agreement between claimant and Foreclosure Recovery Inc.
  • Signed Assignment of Interest OR Limited Power of Attorney
  • Certified Death Certificate of the property owner (must be certified copy, not photocopy)
  • Government-issued photo ID of the claimant (heir, executor, or administrator)
  • Copy of the property deed showing the deceased as owner
  • State-specific claim form (completed and signed)

9.2 Additional Documents by Situation

Situation Additional Documents Required Where to Get Them
Will exists + Probate completed Letters Testamentary; Copy of the will; Court order appointing executor Probate court in the jurisdiction where the estate was administered
Will exists + No probate Copy of the will; Small estate affidavit (if state allows); Notarized affidavit of heirship Family attorney or the probate court clerk's office can provide affidavit forms
No will + Probate completed Letters of Administration; Court order appointing administrator Probate court
No will + No probate Notarized Affidavit of Heirship; Proof of relationship (birth certificate, marriage certificate); Small estate affidavit (if state allows and amount qualifies) Notary public + vital records office
Surviving spouse (community property state) Marriage certificate; Community property documentation; Spousal affidavit Vital records office + county recorder (for property docs)

9.3 Explaining Documents to the Heir

Most heirs have never heard of "Letters Testamentary" or an "Affidavit of Heirship." Part of your job is explaining what these documents are and where to get them. Here are plain-English explanations you can use:

Plain-English Document Explanations

Letters Testamentary: "This is a document from the probate court that officially says you are the executor of the estate. It gives you the legal authority to act on behalf of the estate. If probate has already been completed, you or the attorney who handled it should have a copy."

Letters of Administration: "Same idea, but this is for situations where there was no will. The court appoints an administrator, and this document proves it."

Affidavit of Heirship: "This is a sworn, notarized statement that identifies who the legal heirs are. It is used when probate was not opened. A notary public can notarize it -- you can usually find one at a bank, UPS store, or shipping center."

Death Certificate: "We need a certified copy, which is the version with the raised seal or stamp from vital records. A photocopy or printout will not be accepted. Most states charge $10-25 for certified copies, and you can order them from the vital records office in the state where the person passed."

Small Estate Affidavit: "Some states allow a simplified process for smaller estates that skips full probate. If the total estate value is below the state threshold, this affidavit can serve as proof of your authority to claim."

10. Probate vs. Non-Probate Situations

Understanding whether probate has occurred is critical because it determines which documents are available and how the claim gets filed.

Factor Probate Completed No Probate
Authority document Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration (already issued by court) Affidavit of Heirship (must be created and notarized)
Ease of filing Simpler -- court has already established authority Harder -- may need to prove relationship and standing
Timeline Faster (documents already exist) Slower (documents must be assembled, sometimes probate must be opened)
Your role Collect existing documents and file Guide the heir through the documentation process, recommend they consult an attorney if needed
Cost to heir Minimal (certified copies only) May vary -- notarization fees, potential attorney fees if probate needs to be opened
When Probate Needs to Be Opened

Agent: "Based on what you have told us, it sounds like probate was not opened for [Deceased's Name]'s estate. In some cases, we can work with a small estate affidavit or affidavit of heirship to file the claim. But depending on the amount involved and the state requirements, it may be necessary to open probate first."

"We are not attorneys and cannot advise you on whether to open probate. What we can tell you is that the surplus amount is [$ Amount], and in our experience, that is worth pursuing. If you need a referral to a probate attorney, we can point you in the right direction."

[Never pressure someone into opening probate. Present the facts and let them decide.]

11. Multiple Heirs -- Managing Group Dynamics

When the deceased owner has multiple heirs, things get complicated fast. Siblings disagree. Estranged family members surface. One heir wants to move forward while another stalls. Here is how to handle it.

Rule: One Point of Contact

Identify one primary heir to work with. This should be the executor, administrator, or the most organized and cooperative family member. All communications flow through this person. You do not want to get caught in the middle of family disputes.

Establishing a Single Point of Contact

Agent: "Since there are multiple heirs involved, what works best is for us to work with one primary contact person who can coordinate with the rest of the family. That keeps things organized and makes sure nothing falls through the cracks. Would you be willing to serve as that point of contact, or is there another family member who would be better suited?"

When Heirs Disagree

Navigating Family Conflict

Agent: "We understand that not everyone in the family may be on the same page, and that is completely normal. Our role is to recover the surplus funds for the estate. How the funds are distributed among the heirs is a separate matter that would be handled according to the will or state inheritance law."

"What we need to move forward is agreement from the person who has legal authority over the estate -- the executor or administrator. If that authority has not been established yet, that would be the first step."

[Do NOT take sides. Do NOT mediate inheritance disputes. If the family cannot agree on who has authority, recommend they consult an attorney.]

Red Flag: Competing Claims If two or more heirs each claim to be the rightful claimant and cannot agree, STOP. Do not file anything. Escalate to Foreclosure Recovery Inc. at (888) 545-8007. Filing with the wrong person's authorization can expose the company to liability.

12. Common Deceased Owner Scenarios & Scripts

Scenario A: Owner Passed Recently (Within 6 Months)

Approach: Extra Gentle, No Urgency

Agent: "We understand this is still a very fresh loss, and we do not want to add to what you are dealing with. We just want the family to know that these funds exist and are available when you are ready. There is no immediate deadline. When the time is right, we are here to help. Would it be okay if we followed up in a few weeks?"

Scenario B: Owner Passed Years Ago, Heir Did Not Know About Funds

Approach: Informative, Build Credibility Fast

Agent: "We are reaching out because when the property at [Address] went through foreclosure and was sold, it actually sold for more than what was owed. That difference -- the surplus -- has been sitting with the state since [Year]. Most people have no idea this money exists because the state is not required to notify former owners or their families. The amount we are showing is approximately [$ Amount]. Does this sound like something you would want to look into?"

Scenario C: Heir Already Has an Attorney Handling the Estate

Approach: Collaborative, Not Competitive

Agent: "That is actually good news -- it means a lot of the legal groundwork has already been done. We are not looking to replace your attorney. What we specialize in is the surplus fund recovery process specifically, which is a different track from general estate administration. In many cases, estate attorneys are happy to have a specialist handle this piece. Would you be comfortable connecting us with your attorney, or would you prefer we send you information that you can share with them?"

Scenario D: The Heir Is Elderly or Has Limited Mobility

Approach: Patient, Offer Maximum Support

Agent: "We want to make this as easy as possible for you. We can handle everything by mail if that is more comfortable. We will send you pre-filled documents that just need your signature. If you have a family member or someone you trust who can help gather documents, that works too. There is no need to go to any office or make multiple trips anywhere."

Part 3
Corporate & LLC Entity Claims

13. Understanding Corporate Entity Claims

When the property owner of record is a company -- an LLC, corporation, partnership, or trust -- the recovery process is different from working with an individual. The company itself is the claimant, but companies do not pick up the phone. You need to find and work with the authorized human being who can act on behalf of that entity.

Types of Entities You Will Encounter

Entity Type Who Has Authority Key Document
LLC (Active) Managing Member or Manager (per Operating Agreement) Articles of Organization + Operating Agreement
LLC (Dissolved) Last Managing Member or designated wind-down agent Articles of Dissolution + Operating Agreement
Corporation (Active) President, CEO, or authorized officer (per corporate resolution) Articles of Incorporation + Corporate Resolution
Corporation (Dissolved) Last officer or director, or court-appointed representative Articles of Dissolution + Last filed Annual Report
Partnership General Partner Partnership Agreement
Trust Trustee (current or successor) Trust Agreement + Trustee Certification

14. Finding the Authorized Person

Step-by-Step Research Process

  1. Check the Secretary of State website for the state where the entity was formed. Search by entity name. This will show you the registered agent, officers/members, formation date, and current status (active, dissolved, revoked, etc.).
  2. Pull the entity's filing documents. Most Secretary of State sites let you download Articles of Organization/Incorporation and Annual Reports for free or a small fee. These name the people involved.
  3. Cross-reference names with skip-trace data. Once you have the names of officers or members, use your skip-trace tools to get current contact information.
  4. Check for a registered agent. If you cannot reach the officers directly, the registered agent is a valid point of contact. They are legally required to accept service and communications on behalf of the entity.
  5. Search for the company online. Sometimes a quick search turns up a company website, LinkedIn profiles of officers, or news articles that give you context about the business and who runs it.
Dissolved Does Not Mean Dead A dissolved LLC or corporation can still file a claim. The company may be administratively dissolved (failed to file annual reports) but the people behind it still exist and still have the right to those surplus funds. Do not skip a lead just because the entity shows as dissolved or revoked.

16. Documents Required -- Corporate/LLC Claims

16.1 Active Company

  • Signed Contingency Agreement (signed by authorized representative on behalf of the entity)
  • Signed Assignment of Interest OR Limited Power of Attorney (entity as grantor)
  • Articles of Organization (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation (Corp) -- filed with Secretary of State
  • Operating Agreement (LLC) or Corporate Resolution (Corp) authorizing the signatory to act on behalf of the entity
  • Certificate of Good Standing (if state requires it) -- obtained from Secretary of State
  • Government-issued photo ID of the person signing
  • EIN verification letter (IRS Form CP-575 or 147C) -- some states require this
  • State-specific claim form (completed and signed by authorized representative)

16.2 Dissolved Company

  • Everything listed above for Active Company, plus:
  • Articles of Dissolution (filed with Secretary of State)
  • Most recent Annual Report or Statement of Information
  • Affidavit or declaration from last officer/member confirming they are authorized to wind down the entity's affairs
  • If company was involuntarily dissolved: documentation showing the individual's standing as last known officer/member

16.3 Trust-Owned Property

  • Full Trust Agreement (or the relevant sections showing trust name, trustee authority, and beneficiaries)
  • Trustee Certification (a notarized statement confirming the trustee's identity and authority)
  • Government-issued photo ID of the trustee
  • If the original trustee has passed: successor trustee documentation and death certificate of original trustee
  • Amendment documents (if the trust has been amended)
Explaining Corporate Documents to the Contact

Operating Agreement: "This is the internal document that spells out who owns the LLC and who has the authority to make decisions. You would have signed it when the LLC was formed."

Corporate Resolution: "This is a formal decision by the company's board or officers that authorizes a specific person to sign documents or take action on behalf of the corporation. If you do not have one, your corporate attorney can draft a simple resolution authorizing you to pursue this claim."

Certificate of Good Standing: "This is a document from the Secretary of State that confirms the company is current on its filings and in good standing. You can usually order one online for $10-25."

Articles of Dissolution: "If the company was officially closed, this is the document that was filed with the state to dissolve it. Even if the company is dissolved, the claim can still be filed -- we just need to show documentation of who had authority when the company closed."

17. Dissolved vs. Active Companies -- How to Handle Each

Decision Tree: Company Status
Is the company ACTIVE with the Secretary of State?
YES: Straightforward. Get the authorized person to sign. Collect standard corporate documents. File the claim.
NO -- It is DISSOLVED.
Was it voluntarily dissolved (the owners chose to close it)?
YES: The last officer/member can still file. Get Articles of Dissolution + a signed affidavit confirming their authority to wind down affairs. Some states allow a limited reinstatement solely for the purpose of pursuing claims.
Was it administratively dissolved (failed to file annual reports)?
YES: The company may be able to reinstate by filing back reports and paying fees. If reinstatement is impractical, the last officer/member can file as the representative of the dissolved entity. Get the last Annual Report as proof of their position.
Has the company been dissolved for over 3 years with no identifiable officer?
YES: This is complex. Escalate to Foreclosure Recovery Inc. at (888) 545-8007. Do not attempt to file without guidance.
Discussing Reinstatement With a Business Owner

Agent: "It looks like [Company Name] was administratively dissolved by the state, which usually happens when annual reports were not filed. The good news is that many states allow you to reinstate the company by filing the back reports and paying a fee. In some cases, this can be done online in a matter of days."

"We can not file the reinstatement for you -- that is something you or your attorney would handle -- but we can tell you that the surplus amount is [$ Amount], and reinstating the company would allow us to move forward with the claim. In our experience, the reinstatement fees are small compared to the recovery amount."

We are a registered asset recovery firm -- you can verify us at usforeclosurerecovery.com or call our main office at (888) 545-8007. What we bring is experience with the specific filing requirements, the documentation standards, and the follow-up process. Universal Communication Toolkit

19. Objection Handling Framework

Every objection follows the same pattern. Use this four-step framework regardless of what the person says:

The A.C.E.S. Framework

  • A -- Acknowledge. Validate what they said. "That is a fair point" or "We completely understand."
  • C -- Clarify. Make sure you understand the real concern. "Just so we are on the same page, is your concern about [X]?"
  • E -- Educate. Provide the relevant fact or explanation that addresses the concern.
  • S -- Steer. Move the conversation forward. "Would it help if we sent you [something specific]?" or "What would make you more comfortable moving forward?"
Example: "I Need to Think About It"

Acknowledge: "Of course -- this is not a small decision, and we would not expect you to rush into anything."

Clarify: "Is there a specific part of the process you would like more information about, or do you just need some time?"

Educate: "We can send you a written summary of everything we discussed, including the claim details, our agreement terms, and how the process works. That way you have it in writing to review."

Steer: "Would it be alright if we followed up next week to see where you stand?"

20. Follow-Up Communication Cadence

Persistence wins claims. But there is a line between persistent and pushy. Follow this schedule:

Touchpoint Timing Method Action
1st Contact Day 1 Phone call Initial outreach using scripts from this guide
2nd Contact Day 1 (if no answer) Voicemail + Text/Email Leave voicemail, send introductory email or text
3rd Contact Day 3 Phone call Follow-up call, reference voicemail/email
4th Contact Day 7 Letter (mail) Formal letter to physical address (see template in Section 22)
5th Contact Day 10 Phone call "Following up on the letter we sent..."
6th Contact Day 14 Final call + email "We want to make sure you have this opportunity before we close our file..."
7th Contact Day 30 Final letter Last attempt. Polite, no pressure. Include contact info.
After 7 Touches With No Response Mark the lead as "unresponsive" in the CRM and move on. Do not continue to call. Revisit in 90 days with one more attempt if the funds are still unclaimed.

23. Do's and Don'ts Quick Reference

Always Do

  • Say "we" -- never "I"
  • Say "the state" -- never "the county"
  • Verify surplus amount before calling
  • Express condolences for deceased claims
  • Let them verify us independently
  • Explain documents in plain English
  • Log every contact in the CRM
  • Honor callback commitments exactly
  • Offer to send information in writing
  • Escalate when you are unsure
  • Be patient with confused or elderly contacts
  • Have the lead record open during calls
  • Recommend an attorney for legal questions

Never Do

  • Use high-pressure sales tactics
  • Provide legal advice of any kind
  • Make promises about timelines or amounts
  • File a claim with incomplete documents
  • Take sides in family disputes
  • Continue calling after 7 touches with no response
  • Guess at surplus amounts -- verify first
  • Say "I know how you feel" to a grieving person
  • Discuss fees before explaining the process
  • Rush someone who is emotional
  • Leave confidential info on voicemails beyond basics
  • Send documents to unverified email addresses
  • File with the wrong heir's authorization

24. Escalation Procedures

Trigger Action

Contact threatens legal action against you or the company End the call politely. Document exactly what was said. Report to (888) 545-8007 immediately.
Suspected fraud (someone impersonating an heir or officer) Do not proceed. Do not confront the person. Document your concerns and escalate.

Heir or officer is a minor A minor cannot sign legal documents. Escalate immediately -- a guardian or court-appointed representative will be needed.
Property had multiple owners (individual + entity, or two separate entities) Escalate to determine how the surplus should be split and who needs to sign.

25. Pre-Submission Quality Assurance Checklist

Before you submit ANY sensitive claim, walk through this entire checklist. Check every item. If anything is missing or unclear, do not submit.

25.1 Deceased Owner Claim -- Pre-Submission

  • Surplus funds verified and amount confirmed
  • Death certificate is a certified copy (raised seal or stamp)
  • Relationship between claimant and deceased is documented and verified
  • Correct authority document obtained (Letters Testamentary, Letters of Administration, or Affidavit of Heirship)
  • All authority documents are current (not expired -- some states set expiration dates on letters)
  • Contingency agreement signed by the proper heir/representative
  • Assignment of Interest or Power of Attorney signed
  • Government-issued ID of the claimant is clear and not expired
  • Property deed copy showing deceased as owner
  • State-specific claim form completed in full -- no blank fields
  • All documents are legible (no blurry scans, no cut-off pages)
  • If multiple heirs: all heirs are identified and the signing heir has proper authority
  • No competing claims or unresolved family disputes
  • All names are spelled consistently across all documents
  • CRM notes are complete and up to date

25.2 Corporate/LLC Claim -- Pre-Submission

  • Surplus funds verified and amount confirmed
  • Entity status confirmed with Secretary of State (active, dissolved, or revoked)
  • Authorized representative identified and verified against corporate documents
  • Articles of Organization/Incorporation obtained
  • Operating Agreement or Corporate Resolution on file
  • If dissolved: Articles of Dissolution and affidavit of authority obtained
  • Contingency agreement signed by authorized representative on behalf of entity
  • Assignment of Interest or Power of Attorney signed
  • Government-issued ID of the signatory is clear and not expired
  • EIN verification (if required by the state)
  • Certificate of Good Standing (if required by the state)
  • State-specific claim form completed in full -- no blank fields
  • Entity name on all documents matches exactly (including punctuation -- "LLC" vs. "L.L.C.")
  • All documents are legible
  • CRM notes are complete and up to date
Zero Tolerance on Incomplete Submissions If your claim package is missing even one document, the state will either reject it or shelve it indefinitely. An incomplete submission does not just delay the claim -- it signals to the state that you are not a credible filer. Get it right the first time.
